<p class="post-summary">As the "<a href="/en-gb/topic/candy-crush/">Candy Crush</a>" developer heads to the <a href="/en-gb/topic/ipo/">IPO</a> counter, some wonder if it has what it takes to keep momentum going.</p>
<p><a href="/en-gb/topic/king-digital-entertainment/">King Digital Entertainment</a> created the undisputed champion of games last year with the highly addictive, virtual, sugar-filled game "Candy Crush." Players number 97 million everyday, which is helping to lead King to the IPO counter later this month.</p>
<p>At the time of the public offering, King expects to be valued at about $7.6 billion, with shares pricing between $21 and $24 per share.</p>
<p>"The valuation is hard to argue when you look at the numbers," said Brian Blair, principal at Wedge Partners. " 'Candy Crush' was a top three game last year and in nearly every geography that had an iPhone app store. Universal appeal and distribution like that is hard to find," he said.</p>
<p>While the company offers 180 other games, it's "Candy Crush" which brings in three-quarters of its revenue. So what happens when players get tired of "Candy Crush"?</p>
<p>Take a look at rival Zynga 's stock price, which has been cut in half since its 2011 IPO as the popularity of its own game "Farmville" fell by the wayside.</p>
<p>"As Rovio has done with 'Angry Birds,' there are many ways to milk a franchise. We've seen this in the console gaming world too with numerous iterations of 'Call of Duty' or the Tony Hawk franchise. So 'Candy Crush' will have a long tail, but investors will want to see new titles and franchises emerge," Blair said.</p>
<p>King is scheduled to list on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol "KING" on March 26.</p>
